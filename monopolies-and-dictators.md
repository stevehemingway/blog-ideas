# Why the U.S. State Department Prefers Dealing with Dictatorships

This is a complex geopolitical topic. Here are some commonly cited reasons why the State Department (and foreign policy apparatus more broadly) may find authoritarian regimes easier to work with for specific objectives—though it's worth noting this is a subject of significant debate among scholars and policymakers:

## Operational Efficiency

- **Centralized decision-making**: One leader or small circle can commit to agreements without legislative ratification delays or domestic political constraints
- **Long-term stability**: No election cycles disrupting negotiations or repudiating predecessor deals
- **Rapid implementation**: Agreements can be executed immediately without bureaucratic or judicial review

## Predictability & Control

- **Consistent interlocutors**: Same rulers for decades vs. rotating democratic governments
- **No public opinion constraints**: Leaders can ignore domestic opposition to unpopular policies
- **Direct patronage relationships**: Aid and incentives flow to specific individuals with guaranteed loyalty

## Specific Policy Objectives

- **Military basing/Access**: Easier to secure long-term leases without legislative scrutiny
- **Intelligence cooperation**: Less oversight, fewer leaks, no congressional committees
- **Economic extraction**: Resource deals can be made with state-owned entities and ruling elites
- **Counter-terrorism**: Extrajudicial actions, renditions, black sites face fewer legal obstacles

## Historical Examples Often Cited

- Cold War alliances with anti-communist dictators (Pinochet, Marcos, Suharto, Shah of Iran)
- Post-9/11 cooperation with Central Asian autocrats for Afghan operations
- Gulf state relationships (Saudi Arabia, UAE) for energy and regional security
- Egypt's military dictatorship maintaining Camp David Accords

## Counterarguments & Nuances

| Criticism of this view | Response |
|------------------------|----------|
| Democracies are more reliable long-term allies | True, but short-term horizons often dominate |
| Publics eventually reject pro-US autocrats | See: Iran 1979, but timelines vary |
| Congress and NGOs push for democratic values | Creates tension with realist foreign policy |
| China's rise challenges this calculus | Competition may reinforce or change dynamics |

## Important Caveats

- This describes structural incentives, not official policy—the U.S. rhetorically supports democracy
- Different administrations weigh these factors differently (idealists vs. realists)
- The "preference" is often situational and contested within the bureaucracy
- Democratic allies (UK, Germany, Japan) remain cornerstone relationships



# Why the UK Government Prefers Negotiating with Large Companies and Big Unions

This analysis examines structural and practical reasons why UK governments (across party lines) tend to favor engagement with major corporate entities and large trade unions over smaller organizations.

## Administrative Efficiency

- **Single point of contact**: One CEO or general secretary vs. hundreds of small business owners or shop stewards
- **Streamlined negotiations**: Fewer parties at the table speeds up policy formulation
- **Reduced coordination costs**: Government can reach comprehensive agreements with fewer meetings
- **Clear implementation chains**: Large organizations have established hierarchies to execute agreements

## Scale and Impact

- **Economic significance**: Decisions with large firms/unions affect more workers, GDP, and tax revenue
- **Macroeconomic stability**: Agreements with big players have measurable national impact
- **Crisis response**: During emergencies (strikes, supply chain issues), scale matters for rapid resolution
- **Regional economic weight**: Large employers or unions may dominate specific geographic areas

## Political Calculus

- **Electoral significance**: Big unions retain (declining) Labour Party influence; big business funds Conservative coffers
- **Media amplification**: Actions by large entities generate headlines; small ones are ignored
- **Lobbying power**: Major firms and unions maintain permanent Westminster presence
- **Revolving door**: Career paths between government, big business, and union leaderships

## Negotiation Dynamics

| Factor | Large Organizations | Small Organizations |
|--------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Bargaining power | Can deliver (or withhold) significant concessions | Limited leverage |
| Enforcement | Internal discipline ensures compliance | Fragmented, harder to bind |
| Expertise | Dedicated policy teams and legal departments | Volunteer-run, resource-constrained |
| Time horizons | Long-term strategic relationships | Often reactive, issue-specific |
| Information quality | Detailed economic data and forecasting | Anecdotal, limited data |

## Historical and Institutional Factors

- **Corporatist legacy**: 1970s "social contract" tradition established big union/government negotiation norms
- **CBI and TUC institutionalization**: Peak bodies recognized as legitimate interlocutors
- **Regulatory capture**: Large incumbents shape rules that favor scale (compliance costs)
- **Whitehall culture**: Civil service trained to engage with established, "credible" partners

## Specific Policy Arenas

### Employment and Industrial Relations
- Minimum wage setting: Low Pay Commission engages major employer/union representatives
- Strike mediation: ACAS focuses on disputes with national impact
- Skills policy: Large employers dominate apprenticeship standards design

### Economic Policy
- Industrial strategy: "Sector deals" require scale to participate
- Procurement: Framework agreements favor large suppliers
- Tax policy: HMRC dedicated teams for major corporations

### Brexit and Trade
- Trade negotiations: Large exporters and multinationals consulted extensively
- Regulatory alignment: Big firms provide impact assessments
- Northern Ireland protocol: Major logistics operators prioritized

## Critiques and Counterarguments

- **Democratic deficit**: Small businesses employ nearly half the workforce but lack voice
- **Innovation stifling**: Incumbent protection disadvantages disruptors
- **Geographic bias**: London/Southeast concentration in access
- **Sectoral blind spots**: Emerging industries without established representation

## Recent Developments

- **Union decline**: Membership halved since 1979 reduces big union leverage
- **Gig economy**: Platform companies resist traditional negotiation frameworks
- **SME advocacy**: Federation of Small Businesses gaining some traction
- **Devolution**: Scottish and Welsh governments experiment with broader consultation

## Conclusion

The preference is structural rather than conspiratorial—governing requires manageable negotiation partners. However, this creates systematic exclusion of smaller voices, with consequences for policy design and democratic legitimacy.
