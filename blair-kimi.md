## Prompt

why is Tony Blair so unpopular in Britain?

## Response

Tony Blair is unpopular in Britain for three main reasons, all of which have fused into a single reputation: “he lied and got rich.”

1. Iraq  
   The 2003 invasion is the emotional core of the resentment. A majority of the public now regards the war as illegal, launched on false pretences (WMD) and dragging Britain into a conflict that cost 179 UK service personnel and hundreds of thousands of Iraqi lives. The Chilcot Inquiry (2016) concluded that Blair exaggerated the threat, ignored warnings about the aftermath and undermined the UN process. For many voters, especially on the centre-left who had trusted Labour to be the anti-war party, this felt like a personal betrayal.

2. “New Labour” and the 2008 crash  
   Blair’s domestic settlement—market-friendly, light-touch financial regulation, PFIs/Academies, mass immigration from EU accession states—was accepted while the economy boomed. After 2008 it looked like a giant Ponzi scheme: banks bailed out with public money, ordinary taxpayers left with the bill, while Blairite ex-ministers moved into lucrative City, consultancy and property roles. The sense that he “got away with it” while communities lost industries, council housing and public services is still raw.

3. Post-office money-making  
   Within months of leaving No 10 Blair set up a commercial consultancy (Tony Blair Associates) and a labyrinthine foundation structure. He reportedly earns £5–20 m a year advising the Kazakh government, Saudi interests, JP Morgan, insurers, property funds, etc. In a country where former PMs traditionally retire to the Lords and write tame memoirs, the scale and speed of the enrichment looks obscene. Every fresh six-figure speaking fee or mansion purchase (his property empire is now worth well over £30 m) is recycled in the press as proof that “he always cared more about money than us.”

Add these together and Blair has no natural constituency left: the left hates him for Iraq and neoliberalism; the right hates him for immigration, “political correctness” and the EU; and the broad middle hates him because the crash destroyed trust that the boom had been real. Even Labour centrists who admire his three election victories keep him at arm’s length, fearing the toxicity. Hence polls regularly find 60-70 % of Britons say they have an unfavourable view of him—one of the lowest favourable ratings of any living ex-PM.
